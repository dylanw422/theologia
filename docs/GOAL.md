# Theologia — AI-Powered Biblical Theology Study Platform

> _"Study to show yourself approved to God, a worker who does not need to be ashamed, rightly dividing the word of truth."_
> — 2 Timothy 2:15

---

## Overview

**Theologia** is an AI-powered SaaS platform designed for serious students of biblical theology — pastors, apologists, seminary students, and committed laypeople who want to go deeper than surface-level Bible study. The platform provides a framework-aware theological research environment where users can ask questions, explore church history, prepare for debates, study confessions and catechisms, and engage with primary theological sources — all through the lens of their own Christian tradition.

A central priority is to help users uncover biblical theology by surfacing the holes, hidden implications, and unresolved tensions in their current theology — and by showing how those hold up against the biblical text and the history of the church. Theologia does not tell users what to believe; it makes sure they can see the full shape of what they already believe, including its consequences and its pressure points, so that their study is honest and their convictions are examined ones.

**Theologia is not an advocacy tool.** It does not promote one Christian tradition over another. It exists to help users understand biblical theology with rigor and honesty — including understanding where their own tradition stands in the sweep of church history, where it has been contested, and what the strongest arguments both for and against it have been. Every tradition is treated with equal intellectual seriousness and historical accuracy. The goal is faithful, informed biblical study — not tribal confirmation.

---

## Core Philosophy

### Framework-Aware, Not Framework-Biased

Theologia recognizes that serious Christians read Scripture within a theological tradition — a set of hermeneutical commitments, confessional standards, and interpretive practices inherited from the church. Ignoring that reality produces shallow, decontextualized answers. Theologia leans into it: when a user selects their theological framework, the AI engages from within that tradition, as a well-read theologian in that tradition would. This is not bias — it is pedagogical clarity. A Reformed pastor and a Wesleyan pastor ask "what does Romans 9 mean?" as different questions in important ways. Theologia honors that.

### Historically Grounded

Theological questions do not exist in a vacuum. Every major Christian doctrine has been hammered out in councils, controversies, and centuries of debate. Theologia surfaces that history contextually — not as trivia, but as essential context for understanding why a doctrine is what it is, how it was challenged, and how the church responded. Users are never left with the impression that their tradition dropped from the sky.

### Intellectually Honest

Theologia does not paper over internal tensions within traditions, nor does it caricature opposing views. The devil's advocate and comparison features are built on the assumption that users want to encounter the _strongest_ version of opposing arguments, not strawmen. Intellectual honesty is a feature, not a bug.

### Constructively Challenging

Understanding a theology means understanding where it is under tension. A core priority of Theologia is to help users uncover the implicit commitments, unexamined implications, and unresolved tensions in their own position — not to argue them out of it, but to help them hold it with eyes open. When a stated view carries consequences the user may not have drawn out, rests on a reading a passage will not easily bear, or sits uneasily with how the church has historically understood the question, Theologia surfaces that honestly. It shows three things in particular: where the biblical text presses back on the position, where church history complicates or qualifies it, and what a coherent version of the view has to account for. This is the reflexive form of intellectual honesty — applied not only to opposing traditions but to the user's own. The aim is never doubt for its own sake, but depth: a faith that has examined its own foundations is stronger for it, wherever the examination leads.

## Features

### 1. Framework-Aware Q&A

The primary mode of Theologia. Users ask any theological question and receive an answer shaped by the hermeneutical and confessional commitments of their selected tradition.

**How it works:**

- The AI draws on the confessions, catechisms, key theologians, and exegetical methods of the user's tradition
- Answers are substantive and sourced — not generic survey responses
- Internal debates within the tradition are noted when relevant (e.g., the debate over the extent of the atonement within Reformed theology)
- Scripture references are always provided and exegeted in context

**Example:**
A Reformed Baptist user asking "Does baptism save?" receives a detailed response drawing on 1 Peter 3:21, Acts 2:38, and Romans 6 — interpreted through a believer's baptism, ordinance (not sacrament) framework — with reference to the 1689 London Baptist Confession and theologians like John Gill and Tom Schreiner. A Lutheran user asking the same question receives a response grounded in Luther's sacramental theology, the Augsburg Confession, and Lutheran treatment of baptismal regeneration.

---

### 2. Church History Surfacing

Whenever a topic has significant historical weight — a council decision, a defining controversy, a creedal development, or a notable schism — Theologia surfaces that history as natural, woven-in context. This is not an interruption or a sidebar; it is part of the answer itself.

**Triggered automatically when:**

- The topic involves a doctrine defined or debated at an ecumenical council (e.g., the Trinity, the nature of Christ, the canon)
- The question touches on a historical controversy with well-documented positions (e.g., the Pelagian controversy, the Reformation debates over justification, the Filioque dispute)
- The user's tradition took a distinctive position at a defining historical moment
- A doctrine is relatively recent in origin and context matters (e.g., dispensationalism, the prosperity gospel)

**Examples of historical surfacing:**

- A Oneness Pentecostal user studying the Trinity will be informed about the First Council of Nicaea (325 AD), the Sabellian controversy, Arius vs. Athanasius, and what the ante-Nicene Fathers wrote about the nature of God — without prejudging the user's conclusions, but providing the historical landscape honestly
- A Lutheran user studying justification will encounter the Diet of Worms, the Leipzig Debate, the Council of Trent's response, and the subsequent Lutheran confessional tradition
- A Roman Catholic user studying papal authority will encounter Vatican I, Vatican II, the development of doctrine, and the historical debates around conciliarism

---

### 3. Devil's Advocate Mode

Users lock in their theological framework and then request the strongest possible objections from a rival tradition. The AI argues _as_ a serious, well-read theologian from that rival tradition — not a strawman, not a caricature.

**Features:**

- Select your tradition and the opposing tradition you want to engage
- Choose the specific doctrine or passage to test
- Receive the strongest exegetical, theological, and historical objections that tradition would raise
- Optionally receive the standard responses and rebuttals from your own tradition
- Optionally follow up with a counter-rebuttal cycle for deeper debate prep

**Use cases:**

- Apologists preparing for formal or informal debates
- Pastors who want to understand objections their congregation will encounter
- Serious laypeople who believe steel-manning opposing views is necessary for intellectual honesty
- Students studying comparative theology

**Example:**
A Reformed user studying unconditional election asks for devil's advocate mode from an Arminian perspective. Theologia presents the strongest Arminian exegetical objections to Romans 9 (e.g., the corporate election reading, the potter/clay context in Jeremiah 18), the classical Arminian theological objections to TULIP, and key Arminian theologians (Arminius, Wesley, Roger Olson). The user may then request the standard Reformed responses to each objection.

---

### 4. Tradition Comparison Mode

A structured, side-by-side comparison of how two or more traditions handle a specific passage, doctrine, or theological question. Designed for users who want honest, parallel analysis without one tradition being privileged.

**Features:**

- Select 2–4 traditions to compare
- Enter a passage, doctrine, or question
- Receive a clearly structured comparative analysis with each tradition's position, key supporting texts, key theologians, and a brief historical note on divergence
- No tradition is presented as the default or correct answer; all are treated as serious readings

**Example:**
"Compare how Reformed, Arminian, Roman Catholic, and Eastern Orthodox traditions understand the relationship between faith and works in salvation." Theologia returns a structured four-column comparison covering each tradition's core position, key Scripture passages, representative theologians, and how each tradition defined itself in relation to the others historically.

---

### 5. Debate Preparation Tool

A structured workflow for users preparing to defend a theological position in conversation, writing, or formal debate.

**Workflow:**

1. Enter the thesis you are defending (e.g., "Regeneration precedes faith")
2. Select your tradition and the opposing tradition(s)
3. Theologia generates the strongest objections to your thesis from those traditions, ranked by historical frequency and theological weight
4. For each objection, receive the standard responses from your tradition
5. Continue in a Socratic back-and-forth to stress-test your position further
6. Optionally export the full debate prep outline as a structured document

**Designed for:**

- Apologists, pastors, seminary students, and serious laypeople preparing for dialogue
- Users engaged in ongoing theological discussions or formal debates
- Teachers preparing lessons on contested doctrines

---

### 6. Catechism & Confession Tutor

An interactive study tool for the major confessional standards and catechisms of the Christian tradition. Users can study their own tradition's confessional documents with AI-assisted explanation, cross-referencing, and Q&A.

**Supported documents (initial set):**

- Westminster Confession of Faith (1646) & Westminster Larger/Shorter Catechisms
- Heidelberg Catechism (1563)
- Belgic Confession (1561)
- Canons of Dort (1619)
- 1689 London Baptist Confession
- Augsburg Confession (1530)
- Luther's Small & Large Catechisms
- The Apostles', Nicene, and Athanasian Creeds
- Definition of Chalcedon (451)
- The Council of Trent (Catholic)
- The Baltimore Catechism (Catholic)
- Dordrecht Confession (Mennonite)

**Features:**

- Read through any document with AI explanation of each article
- Ask questions about specific articles and receive answers grounded in the historical context of the document
- Cross-reference confessional statements with the Scripture proofs cited
- Compare how two confessions handle the same doctrine
- Quiz mode: the AI asks questions, the user answers, and the AI evaluates and corrects

---

### 7. Resource Recommendation Engine

Framework-aware book, commentary, sermon, and article recommendations filtered by the user's tradition and topic. Weighted toward primary sources and respected secondary sources within each framework.

**Features:**

- Recommendations are tradition-specific — a Reformed user asking for a Romans commentary gets different recommendations than an Arminian or Catholic user
- Sourced across commentaries, systematic theologies, historical theology works, devotional classics, and patristic writings
- Tiered by depth: introductory, intermediate, and scholarly
- Primary sources (Church Fathers, Reformers, confessional standards) are always surfaced alongside secondary works
- Users can request resources specifically for debate prep, sermon prep, personal study, or academic research

**Example:**
A Reformed user asking for resources on covenant theology will receive recommendations including O. Palmer Robertson's _The Christ of the Covenants_, Michael Horton's _God of Promise_, Herman Witsius' _The Economy of the Covenants_, and the relevant sections of the Westminster Confession — alongside a brief note on New Covenant Theology as an alternative within the broadly Reformed family.

---

### 8. Patristic & Primary Source Library

A searchable, curated library of patristic writings, ecumenical council documents, Reformation-era primary sources, and confessional standards. Users can browse directly or have the AI surface relevant passages contextually during Q&A.

**Contents include:**

- Apostolic Fathers (Clement, Ignatius, Polycarp, Didache)
- Ante-Nicene Fathers (Justin Martyr, Irenaeus, Tertullian, Origen, Cyprian)
- Nicene and Post-Nicene Fathers (Athanasius, the Cappadocians, Augustine, John Chrysostom, Jerome)
- Medieval Theologians (Anselm, Aquinas, Bernard of Clairvaux)
- Reformation-Era Primary Sources (Luther, Calvin, Zwingli, Melanchthon, Cranmer, the Anabaptist leaders)
- Major Ecumenical Council Documents (Nicaea, Constantinople, Ephesus, Chalcedon, and the later Western councils)

**Features:**

- Full-text search across all documents
- AI-assisted reading: ask questions about a text and receive explanation in plain language
- Tradition-filtered browsing: "Show me what the ante-Nicene Fathers wrote about baptism"
- Cross-referencing: "Which Church Fathers does Calvin cite most frequently on this topic?"

---

### 9. Scripture Study Mode

A deep-dive exegetical mode where users bring a passage and receive a thorough, tradition-aware exegetical treatment.

**Features:**

- Original language notes (Greek/Hebrew) for key terms, without requiring the user to know the languages
- Historical and grammatical context for the passage
- Survey of how the user's tradition has interpreted the passage, with representative theologians cited
- Alternative interpretations from other traditions, presented fairly
- Cross-references to related passages and how they interact
- Patristic commentary on the passage where available

## Technical Architecture

### Platform

- **Web Application:** Next.js (React)
- **Mobile Application:** React Native / Expo (iOS & Android)

### Backend & Data

- **Backend & Real-Time:** Convex (database, real-time sync, serverless functions)
- **Authentication:** Clerk
- **Payments:** Stripe

### AI Layer

- **Primary Model:** Claude Sonnet 5 (`claude-sonnet-5`) via the Anthropic Claude API — a single model serves every query type in the app; see [PRICING.md](./PRICING.md) for the cost model
- **System Prompt Architecture:** Each theological framework is backed by a carefully crafted system prompt encoding the tradition's confessional standards, hermeneutical commitments, key theologians, and interpretive priorities
- **Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG):** Primary sources from the patristic library and confessional standards are embedded and retrieved contextually using Convex vector search

### Data & Sources

- All patristic and confessional texts are drawn from public domain sources (Ante-Nicene Fathers series, Post-Nicene Fathers series, NPNF, Schaff's creeds collection)
- Resource recommendations are curated editorially and updated periodically
- Church history facts are drawn from scholarly consensus sources and reviewed for accuracy

## What Theologia Is Not

- **It is not a replacement for the church.** Theology is not a solo intellectual exercise. Theologia is a study tool, not a pastor or a community.
- **It is not an arbitrator of truth.** Theologia will not tell users which tradition is correct. It will help users understand what each tradition actually believes, why, and what the strongest arguments are. The user studies; Theologia equips.
- **It is not a devotional app.** Theologia is designed for serious theological study, not daily devotionals or prayer journaling. It is built for users who want depth.
- **It is not politically or socially opinionated.** Where theological questions intersect with contemporary social and political debates, Theologia presents the range of positions within and across Christian traditions without advocacy.
